# Governance & Doctrine

US-SPURS is intended to operate with clear strategic direction, disciplined execution, and mission accountability across technical, operational, and administrative domains.

## Governance Objectives

The governance model exists to ensure that US-SPURS remains:

- mission-driven
- operationally disciplined
- technically credible
- accountable in execution
- resilient under stress
- adaptable without losing strategic coherence

## Governance Structure

US-SPURS governance is built on layered oversight.

### Strategic Governance

Strategic governance establishes agency direction, long-term priorities, mission authorization standards, and institutional principles.

### Operational Governance

Operational governance ensures that missions are planned, approved, coordinated, executed, reviewed, and improved in a consistent way.

### Technical Governance

Technical governance defines standards for systems architecture, security, interoperability, data management, automation, and engineering quality.

### Administrative Governance

Administrative governance covers finance, procurement, personnel, compliance, and internal support controls.

## Doctrine Principles

US-SPURS doctrine should be grounded in a small number of durable principles.

### 1. Mission First

Programs, systems, and processes exist to support outcomes in the real world, not bureaucracy for its own sake.

### 2. Human Impact Matters

US-SPURS should prioritize improvements to safety, stability, resilience, and public well-being.

### 3. Build for Reality

Technology, infrastructure, and processes should be designed for real deployment conditions, not idealized lab environments.

### 4. Coordinate Across Domains

Engineering, logistics, communications, AI, and field operations should function as an integrated system.

### 5. Move Fast, But With Structure

Operational speed is essential, but speed without governance creates fragility. US-SPURS should move rapidly within disciplined frameworks.

### 6. Learn and Improve Continuously

Every mission, pilot, and deployment should produce lessons that strengthen future performance.

## Authority Model

US-SPURS should maintain clarity around who has authority to:

- set priorities
- authorize missions
- approve expenditures
- establish technical standards
- accept operational risk
- approve deployment of new systems
- conduct after-action review and corrective action

## Decision Framework

US-SPURS decisions should generally be evaluated against five questions:

1. Does this advance mission outcomes?
2. Does this reduce risk or improve resilience?
3. Is it operationally practical?
4. Can it scale or be repeated effectively?
5. Can it be governed, maintained, and audited responsibly?

## Accountability Mechanisms

Governance should include formal accountability in areas such as:

- mission approvals
- change management
- technical review
- security review
- after-action reporting
- budget and procurement controls
- policy and doctrine revision
